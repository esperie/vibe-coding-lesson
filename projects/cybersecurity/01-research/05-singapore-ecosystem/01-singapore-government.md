## Cyber Security Agency of Singapore (CSA)

### Overview

The Cyber Security Agency of Singapore (CSA) was established in **2015** to keep Singapore's cyberspace safe and secure. CSA is part of the **Prime Minister's Office** and is managed by the **Ministry of Digital Development and Information (MDDI)**.

> **10th Anniversary (2025)**: CSA celebrated its 10th anniversary in 2025, marking a decade of strengthening Singapore's cyber defence.

### CSA's Mission and Responsibilities

CSA's core functions include:

1. **National Cybersecurity Oversight**: Maintains oversight of national cybersecurity functions
2. **CII Protection**: Works with sector leads to protect Critical Information Infrastructure
3. **Stakeholder Engagement**: Engages with various stakeholders to heighten cyber security awareness
4. **Ecosystem Development**: Builds a vibrant cybersecurity ecosystem supported by a robust workforce
5. **International Cooperation**: Represents Singapore in international cybersecurity forums

### Organizational Structure

CSA operates across several key divisions:

| Division | Focus Area |
|----------|------------|
| **Policy and Legislation** | Cybersecurity Act, regulatory frameworks |
| **CII Protection** | Sector oversight, incident response |
| **Operations** | SingCERT, threat intelligence |
| **Talent and Ecosystem** | SG Cyber Talent, industry development |
| **International** | Bilateral relations, multilateral forums |

### Key CSA Initiatives (2024-2025)

#### Research and Innovation
- **CyberSG R&D Programme Office (CRPO)**: S$62 million funding over 4 years
- **CyberCall 2024**: S$3 million awarded to 5 cybersecurity companies
- **Translation and Innovation Grant 2025**: S$13.2 million across 13 research projects
- **CyberSG TIG Centre**: S$20 million joint initiative with NUS

#### Talent Development
- **SG Cyber Talent**: Comprehensive talent pipeline programs
- **SG Cyber Youth Programme**: Trained 2,100+ secondary students by June 2024
- **CyberBoost/CyberGrowth**: Support for startups and entrepreneurs

#### Public Awareness
- **Cyber Safe Programmes**: For students, seniors, and general public
- **Cyber Essentials**: Certification for small and medium enterprises
- **Cyber Trust**: Advanced certification mark
- **IoT Labelling Scheme**: Security ratings for smart devices

## Singapore Cybersecurity Strategy 2021

### Strategic Framework

Singapore's Cybersecurity Strategy 2021 is built upon **three strategic pillars** supported by **two foundational enablers**:

#### Strategic Pillars

| Pillar | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Building Resilient Infrastructure** | Protecting critical systems and essential services |
| **Enabling a Safer Cyberspace** | Creating secure environment for businesses and individuals |
| **Enhancing International Cooperation** | Collaborating globally on cybersecurity challenges |

#### Foundational Enablers

| Enabler | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Developing a Vibrant Cybersecurity Ecosystem** | Building local capabilities, supporting startups |
| **Growing a Robust Cyber Talent Pipeline** | Education, training, workforce development |

1. **Legislation Enhancement**: Cybersecurity Act amendments passed in 2024

## Critical Information Infrastructure (CII) Protection

### The 11 CII Sectors

Singapore designates 11 sectors as Critical Information Infrastructure:

| # | Sector | Regulator/Lead Agency |
|---|--------|----------------------|
| 1 | **Energy** | Energy Market Authority (EMA) |
| 2 | **Water** | PUB |
| 3 | **Banking and Finance** | Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) |
| 4 | **Healthcare** | Ministry of Health (MOH) |
| 5 | **Government** | GovTech |
| 6 | **Infocomm** | IMDA |
| 7 | **Media** | IMDA |
| 8 | **Security and Emergency Services** | Ministry of Home Affairs (MHA) |
| 9 | **Aviation** | Civil Aviation Authority of Singapore (CAAS) |
| 10 | **Land Transport** | Land Transport Authority (LTA) |
| 11 | **Maritime** | Maritime and Port Authority (MPA) |

### CII Owner Obligations

Under the Cybersecurity Act, CII owners must:

1. **Comply with Codes of Practice**: Implement mandatory cybersecurity measures
2. **Conduct Audits**: Regular security audits and compliance assessments
3. **Report Incidents**: Notify CSA within **2 hours** of discovering relevant incidents
4. **Participate in Exercises**: Engage in national cyber defence exercises

### Enhanced Incident Reporting (2024 Amendments)

CII owners must now report:
- Incidents involving suspected **Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs)**
- Incidents disrupting essential services, even from non-interconnected systems
- Submit root cause analysis within **14 days** of incident discovery

### Threat Landscape for CII

- **4x increase** in suspected APT attacks on Singapore from 2021 to 2024
- Primary threat: **UNC3886** - state-sponsored APT targeting critical infrastructure
- Key targets: Government, telecommunications, energy, banking sectors

## Cybersecurity Act and 2024 Amendments

### Original Cybersecurity Act (2018)

The Cybersecurity Act established Singapore's legal framework for:
- Oversight and maintenance of national cybersecurity
- Regulation of CII owners
- Incident reporting requirements
- Licensing of cybersecurity service providers

### 2024 Amendments (Effective October 31, 2025)

The Cybersecurity (Amendment) Act was passed in Parliament on **May 7, 2024** and came into force on **October 31, 2025**.

#### New Regulated Categories

| Category | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Systems of Temporary Cybersecurity Concern (STCC)** | Systems critical to Singapore designated for a limited period due to high cyberattack risk |
| **Entities of Special Cybersecurity Interest (ESCI)** | Organizations of particular importance to national security |
| **Foundational Digital Infrastructure (FDI)** | Critical digital infrastructure like data centres and cloud services |
| **Provider-Owned CII (PO CII)** | CII located wholly outside Singapore but owned by designated providers |

#### Key Regulatory Changes

1. **Expanded Scope**: Beyond traditional CII to include cloud services, data centres
2. **Enhanced Reporting**: 2-hour notification requirement for APT-related incidents
3. **Third-Party Oversight**: Obligations extend to service providers
4. **Temporary Designations**: Ability to designate systems during periods of heightened threat

### Compliance Timeline

| Date | Milestone |
|------|-----------|
| May 7, 2024 | Amendment Act passed in Parliament |
| October 31, 2025 | Key provisions come into force |
| Ongoing | Phased implementation of new categories |

## GovTech Cybersecurity Programs

### Government Cybersecurity Operations Centre (GCSOC)

The GCSOC is an integrated initiative that aims to:
- Upgrade government's monitoring and detection technologies
- Automate and augment cyber threat detection and response
- Develop capabilities to proactively hunt for sophisticated threats

### Government Bug Bounty Programme (GBBP)

Launched in **December 2018**, the GBBP has:
- Uncovered potential vulnerabilities across government systems
- Engaged ethical hackers to identify weaknesses before exploitation
- Become a model for government security testing globally

### STACKx Cybersecurity Conference

GovTech's annual cybersecurity conference focuses on:
- Proactive security testing approaches
- Government technology stack security
- Public sector digital transformation security

> **STACKx Cybersecurity 2025**: Held April 15, 2025 at Sands Expo and Convention Centre

### CVE Numbering Authority (CNA)

GovTech is the **first Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures (CVE) Numbering Authority in Singapore**, enabling:
- Prompt assignment of CVE numbers to identified vulnerabilities
- Quick remediation across different organisations and platforms

### Digital Academy Training

GovTech elevates cyber competencies through:
- Cybersecurity training programs for public service officers
- Regular phishing exercises
- Quizzes and awareness campaigns
- Upskilling courses for cybersecurity specialists

### AI Integration in Cybersecurity

GovTech's AI cybersecurity initiatives:
- Incorporating AI solutions into Singapore Government Tech Stack (SGTS)
- Offering secure-by-design AI services for agencies
- Developing AI-driven threat detection capabilities

## IM8 Government Security Standards

### Overview

**Instructional Manual 8 (IM8)** is the management tool used in government to safeguard government ICT & Smart Systems (ICT&SS) assets.

### Governance

- **Governed by**: Smart Nation and Digital Government Group (SNDGG)
- **Purpose**: Sets foundational security and governance policies for all public sector IT systems
- **Scope**: Dictates how systems should be architected, managed, and secured

### Key Components

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **IM8 Policy on Security** | Tailored security hygiene practices based on system classification and criticality |
| **Digital Service Standards (DSS)** | Standards for digital service development |
| **Third-Party Management (TPM)** | Guidelines for managing external vendors |
| **Data Policies** | Data handling and protection requirements |

### Implementation Support

SNDGG supports IM8 implementation by:
- Providing consultancy services
- Deploying Chief Information Security Officers (CISOs) across government
- Conducting regular penetration testing on government systems

### 2024 Reforms

Key reforms announced in 2024:

1. **SaaS Adoption**: Easier for agencies to adopt SaaS applications hosted outside Government Commercial Cloud (GCC) for low-risk use cases
2. **Risk-Based Approach**: Differentiated treatment based on risk impact levels
3. **OSCAL Adoption**: Using Open Security Controls Assessment Language for standardized documentation

### System Security Compliance Test (SSCT)

The SSCT is a **mandatory audit** vendors must pass before:
- Connecting to government networks
- Going live for government projects involving IP-connected systems

The DIA will:
- Make digital systems (data centres, cloud services) more reliable and secure

## OT Cybersecurity Masterplan 2024

### Background

The **Operational Technology (OT) Cybersecurity Masterplan** was first launched in 2019 to enhance the security and resilience of organizations operating industrial control systems (ICS) and OT technologies.

### OT Masterplan 2024 Launch

Launched on **August 20, 2024** by Minister Josephine Teo at the fourth OTCEP Forum, the updated Masterplan reflects:
- Evolving maturity of the OT ecosystem
- Dynamic nature of cyber threats targeting OT systems
- Geopolitical and technological shifts

### Key Focus Areas

#### 1. People
- Enhance OT cybersecurity talent pipeline
- Professional development programs
- OT Cybersecurity Competency Framework (OTCCF)

#### 2. Process
- Enhanced information sharing and reporting
- Streamlined collaboration with OT-ISAC
- Comprehensive threat intelligence ecosystem

#### 3. Technology
- Secure-by-Deployment principles
- Lifecycle management of OT systems
- Adoption of cybersecurity best practices

### Key Initiatives

| Initiative | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **OT-ISAC** | OT Cybersecurity Information Sharing and Analysis Center |
| **OTCEP** | OT Cybersecurity Expert Panel |
| **OTCCF** | OT Cybersecurity Competency Framework |
| **OT Masterclasses** | Advanced training on ICS fundamentals, architecture, incident response |

### Industry Commitment

At the Masterplan launch, **14 organizations** (OEMs and cybersecurity solution providers) committed to adopt Secure-by-Deployment principles.

### Beyond CII

The Masterplan 2024 expands focus to:
- Uplift OT cybersecurity resilience beyond traditional CII sectors
- Establish OT Cybersecurity Centre of Excellence
- Support OT SMEs with resources and guidance

## MAS Financial Sector Cybersecurity

### Technology Risk Management (TRM) Framework

The MAS TRM Guidelines, first introduced in **2001** and most recently revised in **January 2021**, help financial institutions safeguard IT systems against technology and cyber risks.

### Key 2024 Regulatory Updates

#### Notice on Technology Risk Management (Effective May 10, 2024)

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| **Critical Systems Identification** | Framework to identify critical systems |
| **High Availability** | Reasonable efforts to maintain high availability |
| **RTO** | Recovery Time Objective of no more than 4 hours |
| **Incident Notification** | Notify MAS within 1 hour of discovering relevant incidents |
| **Root Cause Report** | Submit within 14 days of incident discovery |

#### Notice on Cyber Hygiene (Effective May 10, 2024)

Requirements for:
- Securing administrative accounts
- Applying security patching
- Establishing baseline security standards
- Deploying network security devices
- Implementing anti-malware measures
- Strengthening user authentication

### MAS CTREX Panel (September 2024)

The **Cyber and Technology Resilience Experts (CTREX) Panel** was established in September 2024 to:
- Advise MAS on emerging technology risks
- Recommend strategies for cyber resilience
- Bring global industry expertise to Singapore's financial sector

**Inaugural Meeting**: April 16, 2025 - Topics included technology resilience, third-party risks, quantum security, digital financial scams.

### Third-Party Risk Management (December 2024)

**MAS Notice 658** (effective December 11, 2024) outlines mandatory requirements for banks regarding third-party risk management.

### Threat Context

- Banks in Singapore faced an average of **1,830 cyberattacks per week** (Dec 2024)
- 63% of phishing attempts in 2023 spoofed banking and financial services sector
- Banking and Financial Services among most targeted sectors

### Quantum Risk Advisory (February 2024)

MAS Circular No. MAS/TCRS/2024/01 urges financial institutions to address cybersecurity risks from quantum computing developments.
