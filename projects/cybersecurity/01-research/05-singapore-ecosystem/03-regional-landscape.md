## ASEAN Cybersecurity Cooperation

### ASEAN Regional CERT

**Launch**: October 16, 2024 (commenced operations)

**Location**: ASEAN-Singapore Cybersecurity Centre of Excellence

**Significance**: First regional CERT of its kind in ASEAN

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Funding** | USD 10.1 million over 10 years |
| **Funder** | Singapore (as ADGMIN Chair) |
| **Agreement** | ASEAN Member States agreed February 2024 |

**Purpose**:
- Cross-border collaboration against cyber threats
- Coordinated incident response across ASEAN
- Information sharing on regional threats

### ASEAN Cybersecurity Frameworks (2024)

Two key agreements signed on **June 10, 2024** by all 10 ASEAN member states:

| Agreement | Purpose |
|-----------|---------|
| **ASEAN CERT Framework** | Establishes CERT coordination mechanism |
| **ASEAN Cybersecurity Capacity Building Programme (ACCP)** | Non-binding agreement to enhance capabilities |

**ACCP Objectives**:
- Share best practices across member states
- Exchange expertise and training
- Access technical assistance from regional and international partners
- Support ASEAN Cybersecurity Strategy 2025 implementation

### 9th ASEAN Ministerial Conference on Cybersecurity (AMCC)

**Date**: October 16, 2024 (part of SICW 2024)

**Outcome**: Reaffirmation of commitment to strengthen regional cyber resilience

**Attendees**: Minister Josephine Teo and representatives from all ASEAN Member States

### ASEAN Cybersecurity Cooperation Strategy 2021-2025

Four focus areas:
1. **Advancing Cyber Readiness**: Building national and regional capabilities
2. **Strengthening and Harmonizing Policies**: Aligned regulatory frameworks
3. **Enhancing Trust in Cyberspace**: Confidence-building measures
4. **Regional Capacity Building**: Training and education programs

## Five Eyes and Intelligence Alliances

### Five Eyes Overview

The **Five Eyes (FVEY)** alliance comprises:
1. United States
2. United Kingdom
3. Canada
4. Australia
5. New Zealand

### Singapore's "Third Party" Status

Based on leaked documents (Edward Snowden, 2013):

- Singapore is a key **"third party"** partner working with Five Eyes
- Singapore is one of the world's biggest digital telecommunications hubs
- Both Singapore and South Korea help US and Australia in tapping fiber-optic cables connecting Asia

> **Important Distinction**: Unlike "second party" members (Five Eyes themselves), "third party" partners are not automatically exempt from intelligence targeting.

### Implications for Singapore

| Aspect | Implication |
|--------|-------------|
| **Intelligence Access** | Access to shared threat intelligence and security information |
| **Cyber Defense** | Cooperation on cyber threats and attribution |
| **Privacy Considerations** | Potential surveillance implications for residents |
| **Economic Espionage** | Protection against state-sponsored cyber espionage |
| **Diplomatic Balance** | Managing relationships with China while cooperating with Western allies |

### Extended Intelligence Networks

Beyond Five Eyes:

| Alliance | Members | Singapore Relationship |
|----------|---------|----------------------|
| **Nine Eyes** | FVEY + Denmark, France, Netherlands, Norway | Indirect cooperation |
| **14 Eyes** | Nine Eyes + Germany, Belgium, Italy, Spain, Sweden | Advisory sharing |

### Cybersecurity Cooperation with Five Eyes Nations

Singapore actively co-signs international cybersecurity guidance with Five Eyes members:

**Joint Publications (2024)**:
| Publication | Date | Partners |
|-------------|------|----------|
| Event Logging and Threat Detection | Aug 22, 2024 | AUS, JP, US, UK, CA, NZ, SG, KR, NL |
| OT Cyber Security Principles | Oct 2, 2024 | AUS, JP, US, UK, CA, NZ, DE, NL, KR |
| APT40 Advisory | 2024 | AUS, US, UK, CA, NZ, DE, KR, JP |

### Singapore's Calibrated Attribution Approach

When Singapore publicly attributed UNC3886 (July 2025):

> Singapore chose a path of "calibrated attribution" - revealing just enough to signal capability and resolve, but not so much as to risk diplomatic rupture with China.

Minister Shanmugam named the actor but stopped short of explicitly attributing to China, illustrating the tension between economic ties and being targets of cyber operations.

### UNC3886 - Primary Threat to Singapore

**Attribution**: State-sponsored APT group (implicitly Chinese)

**Active Since**: Late 2021

**First Public Naming**: July 18, 2025 by Minister K Shanmugam

**Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs)**:
- Living off the Land (LoTL) techniques
- Zero-day exploits
- Focus on high-value strategic targets
- Critical infrastructure targeting

**Singapore Targets**:
- Critical national infrastructure
- Banking systems
- Energy grids
- Water networks
- Transport hubs

**Scale**: 4x increase in suspected APT incidents from 2021-2024

### Stately Taurus (Mustang Panda) - March 2024 Campaign

**Discovered by**: Palo Alto Networks Unit 42

**Timing**: During ASEAN-Australia Special Summit (March 4-6, 2024)

**Targets**: Entities in Myanmar, Philippines, Japan, Singapore

**Method**: Two malware packages leveraged to target Asian countries

### TAG-43 Campaign (Oct 2023 - Jan 2024)

**Targets**:
- ASEAN government organizations
- Non-Governmental Organizations
- Media outlets

**Method**: Compromised edge devices (firewalls, routers, network devices)

### Singtel Breach (November 2024)

**Attacker**: Volt Typhoon (Chinese-linked)

**Context**:
- International reporters (not government) made the attribution
- May have been precursor to broader campaigns against US telecoms
- Highlights risks to telecommunications sector

## Cross-Border Cyber Threats

### Supply Chain as Attack Vector

**ASEAN Context**:
- Deep reliance on third-party vendors and open-source software
- Supply chain attacks are insidious and cascading
- Global supply chains create entry points for nation-state actors

**Strategic Targeting**:
As ASEAN nations position themselves as critical nodes in global supply chains for:
- Semiconductors (USD 200+ billion by 2032)
- EV batteries

Threat actors systematically penetrate:
- Government networks
- Telecommunications infrastructure
- Industrial targets

**Objectives**:
- Extract intellectual property
- Monitor industrial policy
- Pre-position for potential disruption operations

### Regional Cybercrime

**Scale**: 82% increase in cybercrime (2021-2022)

**Key Vectors**:
- Cross-border phishing campaigns
- Ransomware-as-a-Service operations
- Business email compromise
- Cryptocurrency-related fraud

### Transnational Scam Operations

**Context**: Southeast Asia hosts large-scale scam compounds, particularly in:
- Cambodia
- Myanmar
- Laos

**Impact on Singapore**:
- S$1.1 billion total scam losses (2024)
- Cross-border scam syndicates target Singaporean residents
- International cooperation required for disruption

### Counter Ransomware Initiative (CRI)

**Members**: 70+ countries

**Singapore's Role**: Pioneering and leading member

**Next Summit**: October 24, 2025 (hosted by Singapore)

**Objectives**:
- Build collective resilience against ransomware
- Disrupt ransomware criminal industry
- Share threat intelligence and best practices

## Key Takeaways for Government Cybersecurity Unit

### Strategic Considerations

1. **Threat Landscape**: Singapore faces sophisticated state-sponsored threats (particularly from China) alongside regional cybercrime
2. **Alliance Dynamics**: Singapore maintains "third party" status with Five Eyes while balancing relationships with China
3. **Regional Leadership**: Singapore leads ASEAN cybersecurity cooperation through CERT hosting and capacity building
4. **Attribution Challenges**: Calibrated attribution approach balances security needs with diplomatic considerations

### Operational Implications

1. **Intelligence Sharing**: Access to Five Eyes-adjacent threat intelligence through partnerships
2. **ASEAN Coordination**: Key role in regional incident response through ASEAN CERT
3. **Cross-Border Operations**: Need for frameworks to address transnational threats
4. **Sector-Specific Threats**: Telecommunications and finance sectors face heightened targeting

### Capability Gaps

1. **Attribution Capacity**: Need for independent attribution capabilities
2. **Regional Response**: ASEAN CERT still maturing
3. **Threat Intelligence**: Dependence on international partners for APT tracking
4. **Workforce**: Regional talent competition
